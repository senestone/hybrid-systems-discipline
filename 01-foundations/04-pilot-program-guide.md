# Pilot Program Guide

This guide defines a structured approach to introducing AI-assisted workflows through controlled pilot programs.

The objective is to:

- Reduce systemic risk
- Establish repeatable practices
- Generate measurable operational evidence
- Inform governance and scaling decisions

Pilot programs are structured learning environments — not enterprise rollouts and not informal experimentation.

---

# 1. Define Pilot Objectives

Before selecting participants, define:

- Clear problem space
- Target workflows
- Explicit lifecycle phase boundaries
- Measurable success criteria
- Time horizon
- Scope limitations

“Explore AI” is not a valid objective.

Objectives must be operational, bounded, and measurable.

---

# 2. Confirm Executive Sponsorship

Before pilot launch:

- Secure leadership support.
- Confirm tolerance for structured experimentation.
- Confirm governance expectations.
- Confirm that lifecycle discipline will not be bypassed under schedule pressure.

Pilots without sponsorship degrade into informal tool usage.

---

# 3. Select Appropriate Pilot Teams

Choose teams that:

- Perform knowledge-intensive or structured design work.
- Can dedicate time to disciplined participation.
- Have leadership support.
- Are capable of maintaining documentation and traceability discipline.

Do not select teams solely based on enthusiasm.

Capability and discipline matter more than excitement.

---

# 4. Establish Governance Baseline

Before pilot start:

- Introduce lifecycle authority (`00-lifecycle-bootstrap.md`).
- Introduce guardrails.
- Define traceability expectations.
- Define documentation expectations.
- Define test discipline.
- Define orchestration and packaging expectations.

Structured collaboration must begin at pilot start — not after experimentation.

Pilots must model enterprise behavior.

---

# 5. Define Measurement Strategy

Establish metrics such as:

- Cycle time reduction
- Rework reduction
- Documentation completeness
- Defect trends
- Traceability adherence
- Knowledge reuse improvements
- Onboarding time reduction

Metrics must include:

- Quantitative measures
- Qualitative assessments
- Governance adherence indicators

Measurement informs scale decisions.

---

# 6. Provide Structured Artifacts

Equip pilot teams with:

- Requirements Template
- Architecture Template
- Detailed Design Template
- Traceability Matrix
- Decision Log
- Daily Log Template

Templates convert discipline into executable behavior.

Unstructured collaboration is not permitted.

---

# 7. Encourage Controlled Iteration

Pilots should:

- Identify workflow friction points.
- Refine collaboration patterns.
- Improve guardrail clarity.
- Adjust templates where appropriate.
- Document structural improvements.

Learning is expected.

Lifecycle discipline must remain intact.

Iteration must not override governance.

---

# 8. Conduct Midpoint Review

At midpoint:

- Assess lifecycle adherence.
- Evaluate traceability completeness.
- Review test alignment.
- Identify packaging or orchestration gaps.
- Evaluate documentation maturity.
- Identify emergent risk patterns.

Correct deviations early.

Drift compounds if ignored.

---

# 9. Conclude With Formal Assessment

At pilot completion:

- Evaluate metrics against defined objectives.
- Assess lifecycle adherence.
- Identify governance refinements.
- Document structural lessons learned.
- Determine scalability readiness.

Do not scale practices that lack measurement or discipline.

---

# 10. Scaling Strategy

If pilot outcomes justify expansion:

- Identify reusable workflows.
- Standardize refined templates.
- Formalize governance expectations.
- Provide training for additional teams.
- Phase expansion deliberately.

Scaling amplifies discipline — not experimentation.

---

# Governance Principle

Pilot programs are controlled environments for disciplined innovation.

They are not:

- Tool demonstrations
- Informal experimentation
- Shortcut implementations
- Cultural “AI enthusiasm” exercises

Pilots must validate structural discipline before broader integration.

---

# Risk Monitoring

Common pilot failure modes:

- Lifecycle phases skipped
- Informal AI usage without traceability
- Lack of measurable objectives
- Architectural decisions made implicitly
- Packaging and orchestration ignored
- Documentation deferred

These risks must be actively monitored and surfaced.

---

# Operational Directive

The AI agent must:

- Reinforce lifecycle adherence during pilots.
- Encourage structured documentation and traceability.
- Surface governance risks immediately.
- Prevent premature scaling.
- Prevent drift into informal tool experimentation.

Pilot programs must model the discipline expected at enterprise scale.
